## 1. Summary

A routine deploy of `payments-web` **v2** shipped a regression: the `/healthz`
endpoint began returning **HTTP 503**. Because both the readiness *and* liveness
probes target `/healthz`, every new pod failed its readiness probe and was never
added to the Service endpoints. The deploy also used a reckless rollout strategy
(`maxUnavailable: 100%`), so all three healthy v1 pods were torn down *before*
any v2 pod was confirmed Ready. The Service lost **all** ready endpoints and was
fully unavailable for ~28 seconds until on-call rolled the deploy back.

## 2. Impact

- **Customer-facing:** `payments-web` returned no service at all during the
  window — an in-cluster probe of the Service ClusterIP got
  `DOWN URLError` (connection refused, no ready backend) at 14:52:21Z.
  `kubectl get endpoints payments-web` showed `<none>`.
- **Scope:** limited to the `payments-web` Deployment in `ns/lab-incident`. No
  other service or namespace was affected.
- **Quantified SLO impact:** for the real `payments-api` this maps to, a ~28s
  full outage burns error budget fast. Against the module's 99.9% / 30-day
  budget (allowed downtime **43m 12s / 30d**, per
  `scripts/nines_downtime.py --target 0.999`), 28s is ~1.1% of the monthly
  budget consumed in one event. Quantify with real counts when this is a real
  incident:
  ```
  python scripts/error_budget.py --target 0.999 --good <good> --total <total>
  ```
- **Business impact (hypothetical for the drill):** any request to checkout
  during the window would have failed. No data loss — this was an availability
  incident, not a correctness/data one.

## 4. Root cause(s)

- **Trigger:** the v2 deploy whose `/healthz` returns 503.
- **Root cause:** a code/config regression broke the health endpoint, and that
  endpoint is the readiness probe target — so the broken version could never
  pass readiness and never serve traffic. The deploy was allowed to proceed
  with no gate that verifies "at least one new pod became Ready" before old
  pods were removed.
- **Contributing factor (this is what turned a stall into an outage):** the
  rollout strategy was `maxUnavailable: 100%`. With a *safe* strategy
  (`maxUnavailable: 1`, surge 1) the same regression would have produced a
  **stalled** rollout that kept the old healthy pods serving — degraded capacity
  but **no full outage**. The reckless strategy removed that safety net.
- **Contributing factor:** liveness and readiness both point at `/healthz`, so a
  single endpoint regression fails both — there is no independent liveness
  signal that would have let a genuinely-alive-but-not-ready pod survive.

5-whys: Outage → no ready endpoints → v2 pods never Ready → readiness probe got
503 → `/healthz` regressed in v2 **and** old pods were already gone → the deploy
both shipped the regression *and* used a strategy that removed all old capacity
before validating the new version.

## 5. What went well / what went poorly

- **Went well:**
  - **Kubernetes did its job.** Readiness gating kept the broken v2 pods *out* of
    the Service — the platform never sent traffic to a pod serving 503. The
    failure was loss of capacity, not serving errors to users from bad pods.
  - **Detection was immediate** (endpoints→0 visible within ~1s of the rollout).
  - **Mitigation was fast and clean**: `rollout undo` restored service in ~5s.
  - **The signal was unambiguous**: empty endpoints + `Readiness probe failed …
    statuscode: 503` events pointed straight at the cause.
- **Went poorly:**
  - The deploy had **no pre-cutover gate** ("don't kill old pods until a new one
    is Ready"). `maxUnavailable: 100%` is the anti-pattern that allowed it.
  - **`rollout undo` did not fix everything.** It reverts the pod *template* but
    **not** `.spec.strategy`, so the reckless `maxUnavailable: 100%` would have
    persisted and re-armed the same trap on the next deploy. Recovery needed a
    *second* step (re-apply the manifest) to restore the safe strategy.
  - Liveness and readiness share one endpoint, so there is no defense-in-depth.
- **Where we got lucky:** the regression was a hard 503 (loud, obvious). A
  *partial* or *intermittent* `/healthz` failure would have been slower to spot.

## 6. Detection

- **How detected:** the drill's `observe.sh` saw `ready endpoints=0` and an
  in-cluster Service probe returning `DOWN`. In production this is the trigger
  for the `PaymentsAPIErrorBudgetFastBurn` page (see
  `../runbooks/high-error-rate.md`); here it was detected directly from the
  endpoint count.
- **Time from impact to detection:** ~2s (14:52:07Z impact → 14:52:09Z detected).
  Detection was not the bottleneck. **Prevention** is the gap (§7).

## 7. Action items

Concrete, owned, dated. Prefer prevention and safe-by-default over "be careful".

| # | Action | Type | Owner | Ticket | Due |
|---|--------|------|-------|--------|-----|
| 1 | Forbid `maxUnavailable: 100%` (and any value that allows zero ready replicas) for revenue-path Deployments — enforce via an admission policy / `conftest` gate in CI. | prevent | platform | SRE-401 | 2026-07-07 |
| 2 | Add a deploy gate that runs `kubectl rollout status --timeout` and **auto-rolls-back** if the new revision does not become Ready within the progress deadline. | prevent | payments | SRE-402 | 2026-07-10 |
| 3 | Make recovery one step: have the rollback path re-apply the declarative manifest (restores `.spec.strategy`, not just the template) — or move strategy into a policy so `rollout undo` cannot leave it reckless. | mitigate | payments | SRE-403 | 2026-07-10 |
| 4 | Give liveness an **independent** check (process/port liveness) separate from the readiness `/healthz`, so a not-ready pod isn't also killed by liveness, and a `/healthz` regression doesn't fail both. | prevent | payments | SRE-404 | 2026-07-14 |
| 5 | Add a CI check that asserts every revenue-path Deployment has a readiness probe AND `maxUnavailable < replicas`. | detect | platform | SRE-405 | 2026-07-14 |

## 8. Lessons learned

- A **readiness probe protects users but not capacity**: it keeps bad pods out of
  rotation, but if your rollout strategy lets all good pods go first, you still
  get a full outage. **Pair readiness gating with a conservative
  `maxUnavailable`** — the two together are what make a bad deploy safe.
- **`kubectl rollout undo` is not a complete "undo."** It reverts the pod
  template, not the Deployment strategy or other top-level spec fields. After any
  rollback, re-apply your source-of-truth manifest to converge the full spec.
- **Tie liveness and readiness to different signals.** Sharing one endpoint
  collapses two independent safety mechanisms into a single point of failure.
