# Compensation Negotiation Guide

> How to negotiate a DevOps/Cloud/SRE offer without leaving money on the table or
> damaging the relationship. Negotiation is **expected** — recruiters plan for it,
> and a respectful, prepared negotiation does not rescind offers. This guide is a
> playbook, not financial advice; the numbers move, the principles don't.

The single most important mindset shift: **the negotiation starts the moment you
apply, not when the offer arrives.** Every signal you send before the offer
(deferring numbers, demonstrating other interest, performing well) shapes the
number you're handed.

---

## The core principles

1. **Almost every offer has room.** The first number is rarely the company's best
   and final; recruiters expect a counter. Not countering leaves money on the table
   ~most of the time.
2. **Leverage = alternatives.** Your strongest lever is a competing offer (or a
   credible pipeline). Second is being a great fit they don't want to lose. Build
   leverage *before* you need it by running interviews in parallel.
3. **Negotiate the whole package, not just base.** Total comp = **base + bonus +
   equity + sign-on + benefits + level**. When base is capped, other levers open.
4. **Be collaborative, not adversarial.** Frame it as "I'm excited; let's find a
   number that works so this is an easy yes," not a fight. You'll work with these
   people.
5. **Never lie.** Don't invent competing offers or inflate current comp — it's
   small world, it can be verified, and it destroys trust. You can be strategic
   without being dishonest.

## Before the offer: set yourself up

### Do NOT give a number first
When asked *"what are your salary expectations?"* early on, **deflect**:

> *"I'm focused on finding the right fit first, and I'm confident we can land on a
> fair number if it's a match. What range has the team budgeted for this role?"*

Whoever says a number first anchors — and you have less information than they do.
If pressed for current comp (illegal to ask in some places), you can decline:
*"I'd rather not anchor on my current comp; I'm looking at the market rate for
this role and level."* If you must give a range, give a researched, slightly-high
band and say "depending on the total package."

### Know your numbers cold
Before any offer, research:
- **Market rate** for the role + level + location: Levels.fyi, Glassdoor,
  Blind, salary surveys, your network. Get a real range, not a wish.
- **Your walk-away number** (the minimum you'll accept) and your **target**
  (realistic-good) and your **stretch** (great). Decide these *before* emotion is
  involved.
- **Level matters more than salary.** Being hired one level higher is worth far
  more over time (comp band, scope, next-promo base) than a one-time salary bump.
  Negotiate level early — it's hardest to change after the offer.

## When the offer arrives

### Step 1: Get it in writing and say thank you — don't react
> *"Thank you so much, I'm really excited about this. Could you send the full
> details in writing — base, bonus, equity, and start date — so I can review the
> whole package? I'll get back to you by [date]."*

**Never accept on the call.** Buy time. Enthusiasm + a pause is the correct first
response, not a yes and not a counter.

### Step 2: Evaluate the whole package
Build a comparison: base, target bonus (and how reliably it pays), equity (vesting
schedule, current value *and* the assumptions), sign-on, PTO, remote flexibility,
on-call comp/expectations, learning budget, 401k match. Equity especially: a big
"$X" number means little without the vesting schedule and a sober view of the
company's stage.

### Step 3: Counter — once, specifically, with a reason
Anchor with a specific number (not a range — ranges get read as your floor),
justify it, and stay warm:

> *"I'm genuinely excited to join and I think I'll have a real impact here. Based
> on my research for this role and level, and the [specific scope / on-call / skills
> I bring], I was hoping we could get the base to **$X**. Is there flexibility
> there?"*

- **Anchor slightly above** your target (they'll likely meet in the middle).
- **Give a reason** (market data, your scope, a competing offer if real) — a
  justified ask is far stronger than a naked number.
- **Counter on the highest-leverage lever.** If base is capped, pivot:
  *"I understand base is constrained at this level — could we close the gap with a
  sign-on bonus or additional equity?"*
- **If you have a competing offer**, use it honestly:
  *"I have another offer at $Y, but you're my first choice — if you can get close on
  total comp, I'll sign today."* (Only say "I'll sign today" if you mean it.)

### Step 4: When they respond
- If they meet it: **get it in writing**, then accept graciously.
- If they partially meet it: decide against your *pre-set* numbers, not your
  in-the-moment adrenaline. One more small ask is usually fine; a third round
  risks goodwill.
- If they can't move base: ask about the other levers, a **6-month comp review**,
  or a higher level. Get any verbal promises **in the written offer**.

## The levers, ranked by typical flexibility

| Lever | Typical flexibility | Notes |
|-------|---------------------|-------|
| **Sign-on bonus** | High | Easiest to grant; one-time, doesn't touch the comp band. Great when base is capped. |
| **Equity** | Medium–High | Often more room here than base, especially at startups. Mind vesting + cliff. |
| **Level / title** | Medium (if early) | Highest long-term value; negotiate *before* the offer hardens. |
| **Base salary** | Low–Medium | Constrained by internal bands; still ask, but know it's the stickiest. |
| **Start date / PTO / remote** | Medium | Cheap for them to give; valuable to you. |
| **6-month review** | Medium | A path to the number when they can't get there now — get it in writing. |
| **Bonus target** | Low | Usually fixed by level; understand payout history. |

## Scripts for hard moments

- **"This is our best and final."** → *"I appreciate that, and I want to make this
  work. Is there any flexibility on a sign-on bonus or equity to close the gap? I'm
  ready to commit if we can get there."* (Test it once; "best and final" is
  sometimes a position, not a fact.)
- **"We need an answer today."** → *"I'm very interested and want to give you a
  real yes — I just need until [reasonable date] to review the written details.
  Can we do that?"* Genuine artificial-urgency is a small yellow flag; a fair
  employer grants a few days.
- **"What's your current salary?"** → *"I'd prefer to focus on the market value of
  this role rather than anchor on my current comp."*
- **They ask you to name a number first.** → Give a researched range and tie it to
  total package, or turn it back: *"What's the band for this role?"*

---

## After you accept

- **Get everything in writing** before resigning your current job — verbal promises
  evaporate.
- **Decline other offers graciously** — keep the door open; it's a small industry.
- **Don't keep negotiating** after you've accepted — it burns the goodwill you just
  built.

---

## Common mistakes

| Mistake | Why it costs you | Do instead |
|---------|------------------|-----------|
| Accepting on the spot | Leaves money + signals you don't value yourself | Thank them, ask for it in writing, take time |
| Giving a number first, early | Anchors low, with less info than them | Deflect; ask their range |
| Not countering at all | Most offers have room you forfeited | Counter once, specifically, with a reason |
| Countering with a range | They hear your floor | Anchor with one specific number |
| Negotiating only base | Misses sign-on/equity/level room | Negotiate total comp; pivot when base is capped |
| Lying about a competing offer | Verifiable; destroys trust | Use only real leverage; be honest |
| Being adversarial | You have to work with these people | Stay warm, collaborative, "let's make this work" |
| Ignoring level | Biggest long-term lever | Negotiate level *before* the offer hardens |
| Endless rounds | Burns goodwill | Counter once, maybe a small second ask, then decide |

> **The asymmetry that makes this worth it:** a 15-minute, polite, prepared
> conversation can be worth thousands of dollars a year, compounded across your
> tenure and every future offer that anchors on it. The downside of a respectful
> counter is almost always just "no." Ask.
